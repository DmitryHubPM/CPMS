1. What The System Is

The Clinical Budgeting and Project Management System is a protocol-driven planning and execution platform for clinical studies.

It starts where clinical trial planning usually starts: with a protocol, a study design, expected subjects, sites, visits, procedures, monitoring assumptions, and operational milestones. Instead of turning those assumptions into a static spreadsheet, the system converts them into structured work units that can be budgeted, scheduled, staffed, completed, evidenced, billed, and monitored.

A clinical trial budget should not be a flat spreadsheet. It should be an operational model of the study.

2. Why It Exists

Clinical studies are usually planned and managed across disconnected tools: spreadsheets, CTMS, EDC, IRT, eTMF, safety systems, payment trackers, project plans, email, and dashboards. This creates repeated setup and constant reconciliation.

  • Budgets are hard to connect to actual work.
  • Actual hours are hard to compare with planned assumptions.
  • Evidence is chased by email.
  • Site payment triggers are unclear.
  • Completed work may not be billable because documents are missing.
  • Change orders are discovered late.
  • Resource needs are not visible early enough.
One protocol setup should drive budget, schedule, work, evidence, payment, resource planning, and performance metrics.

3. Clinical Budgeting Workflow

The budgeting workflow begins with protocol interpretation. The user identifies activities that drive cost: site start-up, investigator meetings, SIVs, subject visits, monitoring, remote review, CRF review, query management, SAE processing, deviation review, CAPA, project management, data management, close-out, and reporting.

Each activity becomes a budget unit with a phase, milestone, unit count, commercial source, date range, role composition, pass-through cost, and investigator grant amount.

If detail is incomplete, the system preserves the unknown remainder as TBD Country or TBD Site. This keeps the budget reconciled while allowing the study to mature.

7. Resource Planning

Resource planning is generated from unit composition and schedules. If one monitoring visit requires 8 CRA hours and 1 PM hour, then 10 visits require 90 planned hours.

The resource schedule should show month, department, role, employee, planned hours, actual hours, FTE, study, country, and site where available.

This helps answer how many people are needed, when they are needed, which roles are overloaded, and how protocol or timeline changes affect staffing.

8. Actuals And Evidence

The system tracks actual execution, not only plan. For each scheduled unit or sub-unit, the user records completion, actual date, completed by, actual hours, evidence type, evidence status, proof link, and validation status.

Evidence may be a TMF document, non-TMF document, EDC reference, IRT reference, CTMS record, SharePoint link, invoice reference, or payment record.

A completed unit is validated only when required evidence exists and the evidence status is Final or Filed.

9. Billing And Payment

The system connects operations and finance. For each unit or sub-unit, it can track billability, payment trigger, investigator payment status, CRO billing status, sponsor reimbursement, pass-through support, and OOS or change order impact.

Payment triggers include completion only, completion plus document filed, completion plus sponsor approval, and manual approval.

Warnings should appear when evidence is missing, documents are only Draft, actual hours exceed plan, or investigator payment has been made before sponsor reimbursement.

10. EVA And Performance

Earned Value Analysis compares plan, completion, and actual cost.

MetricMeaning

SPIEarned Value / Planned Value
CPIEarned Value / Actual Cost

This is especially useful because a clinical task may be operationally completed but not yet validated, billable, or reimbursed.

11. Contract, Change Order, And OOS

Every budget unit should have a commercial source.

Contract UnitCO1CO2CO3OOS1OOS2OOS3

Contract Unit means baseline contracted scope. CO means approved or planned change order scope. OOS means out-of-scope work not yet converted to a change order.

The contract module should support a work order backbone, commercial budget summary, change order register, OOS register, PM plan, monitoring plan, and SOP/TMF placeholders.

12. End-To-End Demo Scenario

  1. Create a new study called My Test Study with Phase 3, 100 subjects, 10 sites, and 2026 dates.
  2. Add a Monitoring Visit unit with 10 visits, pass-through 900 per unit, and no investigator grant.
  3. Add CRA II at 8 hours x 100/hour and Project Manager at 1 hour x 150/hour.
  4. Confirm planned budget equals 18,500, labor equals 9,500, planned hours equal 90, and FTE months equal 0.56.
  5. Split countries into France 3, Switzerland 3, and Italy 4.
  6. Assign CRA II to Maya Chen and Project Manager to Jonas Keller.
  7. Generate schedule, then override several exact planned dates.
  8. Mark two visits completed and update actual hours.
  9. File evidence for one visit and leave the second visit as Draft to show validation warnings.
  10. Update payment status and show sponsor reimbursement warning.
  11. Review Budget, Resources, Cash Flow, Timesheets, Evidence, EVA, and Contracts & Docs.

13. Future Development Roadmap

Phase 1: Working Demonstrator

Study creation, budget units, country/site split, composition, cost calculations, resource schedule, evidence, payment, EVA, and document backbones.

Phase 2: Persistent Multi-Study Application

Database persistence, user-created studies, scenarios, import/export, validation, and help system.

Phase 3: Workflow And Evidence Layer

Sub-units, workflow steps, owners, due dates, TMF/non-TMF evidence, status approvals, and escalation rules.

Phase 4: Protocol Schedule Engine

Visits, procedures, labs, drug administration, CRF pages, SDV/SDR workload, safety review, deviations, and CAPA triggers.

Phase 5: Integrations

EDC, IRT, CTMS, eTMF, safety database, finance, HR/resource planning, and document management.

Phase 6: Regulated Enterprise Platform

Authentication, role-based access, audit trail, data integrity, e-signature, validation package, SOP alignment, security, and inspection readiness.
